Choosing Right

Third-Party vs Comprehensive

This is the real choice for two-wheeler insurance — not a "which lender" question like a loan, but "how much of your own risk do you want covered."

Third-Party Only

  • Legally mandatory — riding without it is a punishable offence
  • Covers damage or injury you cause to others and their property
  • Rate is fixed by IRDAI — same across every insurer
  • Does not cover your own bike at all, even if you're not at fault

Comprehensive

  • Includes third-party cover plus own-damage protection
  • Covers theft, fire, accident damage, and natural disasters to your bike
  • Premium varies by insurer, IDV, vehicle age, and add-ons chosen
  • Only cover type where No Claim Bonus actually reduces your premium

⚠️ If your bike is financed or under 3-4 years old, comprehensive is usually worth the extra cost — the payout on a total loss or theft claim can be many times your annual premium. Tell us your bike's age and value and we'll tell you honestly whether comprehensive makes sense for you.

*Personal Accident cover for the owner-driver (₹15 lakh) is compulsory under the Motor Vehicles Act and is bundled into your premium by default — you can only skip it if you already hold an equivalent PA cover elsewhere and formally declare it. Pillion (co-passenger) cover is not automatically included even under comprehensive — it's a separate, genuinely optional add-on most riders don't realize they're missing until it's too late.

A concrete example: a 150cc bike with a ₹70,000 IDV might cost around ₹714/year for third-party only, versus roughly ₹3,500-4,500/year comprehensive depending on insurer, NCB, and add-ons — a difference of about ₹2,800-3,800 per year. But if that bike is stolen, third-party pays you nothing toward replacing it, while comprehensive could pay close to its ₹70,000 IDV. Whether that trade-off is worth it depends on how replaceable ₹70,000 is for you right now.

Who Should Buy What — Not Just Features, Your Actual Situation

Third-Party Only Makes Sense If:

  • Your bike is worth roughly ₹20,000-30,000 or less
  • You ride rarely — a backup bike, not a daily commuter
  • You could comfortably pay for repairs or a replacement out of pocket
  • The bike is fully paid off, no loan tied to it

Comprehensive Is Worth It If:

  • The bike is new or under 3-4 years old
  • You ride it daily — your main mode of transport
  • There's an outstanding loan against it (most lenders require this anyway)
  • You live or park in a higher theft-risk area

No Claim Bonus

Your NCB Ladder — And Why It's Worth Protecting

No Claim Bonus is a discount on your own-damage premium for every claim-free year — it does not apply to the third-party portion. It resets to zero the moment you make an own-damage claim, no matter how many years you'd built up.

What Actually Moves Your Premium

What Insurers Actually Check

Engine Capacity

Third-party rates step up in fixed IRDAI slabs by CC. Own-damage premium also generally rises with engine size and vehicle value.

Vehicle Age & IDV

IDV (Insured Declared Value) depreciates every year — a 5-year-old bike has a lower IDV, and therefore a lower own-damage premium, than a new one.

No Claim Bonus

Every claim-free year reduces your own-damage premium — up to 50% by year five. This is the single biggest lever you control.

Add-Ons Selected

Zero depreciation, roadside assistance, and engine protection each add a small premium but can significantly increase your claim payout.

The Process

How a Claim Actually Works

Inform the Insurer Immediately

Most policies require notification within 24-48 hours of an accident or theft — delays can complicate or weaken your claim.

Choose Cashless or Reimbursement

Cashless repair at a network garage means the insurer settles directly with the garage. Reimbursement means you pay first and claim the amount back — cashless is usually faster and less hassle.

File an FIR If Required

Theft, third-party injury, or significant accidents typically require a police FIR — this is often mandatory for the claim to be processed at all.

Submit Documents and Get Surveyed

The insurer's surveyor assesses the damage before approving the repair estimate — keep photos and repair estimates ready to speed this up.

Know What Add-Ons Cover Before You Claim

Zero depreciation cover means no deduction for wear on replaced parts — without it, your payout is reduced by a depreciation percentage based on part age.

Documents to keep ready: policy copy, RC (registration certificate), driving license, FIR copy (for theft/major accidents), repair estimate or bills, and photos of the damage taken immediately after the incident.
